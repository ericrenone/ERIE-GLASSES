## Part II · The Four Operations as the Ecological col(F) of Lived Reality

The ERIE — GLASSES framework holds that the lived human scene — the scene of a body moving through a social and spatial world — does not decompose into pixels or app icons or feed items or AI chat responses. It decomposes into a small number of ecologically sufficient predicates: operations on the combined photon field and noöspheric data stream that produce, when active, outputs that are sufficient statistics for decisions the wearer must make. The ERIE-Glasses waveguide computes exactly four such predicates before delivering any signal to conscious attention:

**Operation 1 — Sustained Gaze Contrast and Scene Registration (col(F)₁)**

A persistent, high-contrast boundary between any two regions of the visual field — physical or AR — with any luminance differential, maintained across a three-hundred-fold range of ambient illumination, anchored to stable physical geometry through the SLAM substrate and HEAR binaural prior. This is the scene model: the low-level col(F) that maintains the registration of AR overlays against the physical world, the foundation against which all social and spatial operations are computed. A building edge, a face boundary, a table surface, a street corner — any persistent physical boundary that the waveguide can anchor an overlay to is col(F)₁. Without it, the AR layer drifts; with it, every overlay lives exactly where the physical world places it, across all head motion, lighting changes, and dynamic occlusions.

**Operation 2 — Net Social Convexity Detection (col(F)₂)**

A human face, a pair of eyes directed toward the wearer, a social agent — biological or AR avatar — occupying a roughly convex region of visual space within a receptive aperture of approximately 7° diameter, moving or stationary, with a sustained response maintained when the agent enters the aperture and ceases independent movement. This is the "accessible person" detector: the col(F)₂ sufficient statistic of the social strike decision. Col(F)₂ fires to a person who is present, attending, and within social interaction range. It does not fire to the background, however socially rich; it does not fire to a face on a phone screen, however intimate the relationship it represents. It fires to the physical co-present social agent in the wearer's actual vicinity — exactly as the frog's convexity detector fired to the independently moving bug and fell silent to the dead fly and the complex background photograph. The response is maintained — a sustained social presence signal — as long as the agent remains within the aperture and ceases independent movement into the wearer's space. It is erased by the deliberate blink-and-aversion erasure gesture, returning the social field to the uninformative prior.

**Operation 3 — Moving Boundary and Shared Motion Detection (col(F)₃)**

Any moving boundary — physical or AR — crossing the receptive aperture of approximately 12°, whether darker or lighter, whether friend or stranger, whether a gesture or a passing car. Speed modulates the discharge rate. The operation fires on onset and on cessation of crossing motion, not to stationary boundaries. The ecological object: any moving agent in the mid-field, any gesture of a co-wearer, any collaborative AR annotation being drawn in real time by a remote or co-present collaborator. Col(F)₃ is the social motion alarm — the general-purpose movement detector that triggers the more specific social convexity assessment of col(F)₂ or the privacy dimming response of col(F)₄. It does not distinguish friend from stranger; that discrimination is handled downstream, by the localized AI that reads the shared tectum's combined output. Col(F)₃ signals only: something is moving in this social field that has not been modeled yet.

**Operation 4 — Net Attention Dimming and Privacy Shield (col(F)₄)**

A rapid, large-area increase in visual field information density — too many AR overlays, too much social information, a privacy-sensitive conversation beginning, an unwanted gaze from outside the shared layer, a shift in context requiring immediate reset of the operative scene. Receptive aperture approximately 15°, response proportional to the rate and spatial extent of information-field increase, myelinated equivalent running at the fastest conduction velocity in the hierarchy. The ecological object: the privacy-or-overload event that requires immediate re-establishment of the wearer's personal information boundary. Col(F)₄ is the escape trigger — the reflex substrate of the "collapse to private mode" behavior that is the wearer's primary social self-protection strategy. A social context that becomes unexpectedly public; a stranger's gaze detected by col(F)₂ as hostile rather than neutral; an AR overlay flooding from an unverified source — all trigger col(F)₄ and initiate the privacy-first response protocol.

**ker(F):** The full un-augmented photon field — the million-point luminance distribution across the retinal surface, the spatial frequency spectrum, the absolute luminance level, the color distribution, the correlations between non-adjacent points — plus the entire noöspheric data lake: every public annotation not within the wearer's current social aperture, every AI inference not yet surfaced to the tectum, every data point in the planetary knowledge graph that has not crossed the ε-threshold into operative relevance. The raw photon field and the infinite noösphere are both ker(F): richly structured, ecologically meaningful in principle, and systematically marginalized by the four operations except where they are needed to instantiate one of the four predicates in the wearer's immediate scene.

The four operations are orthogonal in ecological function. Col(F)₁ operates on the static physical scene; col(F)₂ and col(F)₃ operate on moving social agents; col(F)₄ operates on the global information field. Col(F)₂ requires a sustained co-present social agent; col(F)₃ fires to any motion event. Col(F)₂ and col(F)₄ have erasure mechanisms; col(F)₁ and col(F)₃ do not. ## Part V · The Conduction Velocity Hierarchy and the ε-Threshold of Social Urgency

The four operations are delivered to the wearer's visual cortex at four distinct latencies, ordered by ecological urgency — the same hierarchy that organizes the frog's four ganglion cell fiber populations by myelination and conduction velocity:

**Col(F)₄ — Privacy Dimming (fastest; ~2 ms end-to-end, myelinated-equivalent photonic path).** The privacy escape response is the most urgent operation in the ERIE-Glasses ecology, exactly as the dimming detector is the most urgent operation in the frog's ecology. The cost of a privacy failure — a sensitive overlay surfaced to an unintended audience, a private conversation exposed to a stranger's AR layer, a facial recognition result surfaced without consent — is immediate, socially catastrophic, and irreversible. The privacy shield fires in under two milliseconds from the col(F)₄ trigger event, faster than any conscious deliberation, at the first signal of information-field overload or hostile gaze. It is the waveguide's fastest path: the fewest optical components between trigger and output.

**Col(F)₃ — Moving Boundary and Shared Motion (~10 ms; fast myelinated-equivalent).** The social motion alarm fires quickly to any moving agent in the mid-field — fast enough that the wearer can initiate a social interaction response, a collaborative gesture acknowledgment, or a threat assessment before the moving agent has closed the social distance. Motion urgency is intermediate: missing a moving agent in the social field is costly, but not as immediately catastrophic as a privacy failure.

**Col(F)₂ — Social Convexity (~50 ms; sustained, medium-latency).** The social strike decision — is this person accessible, co-attending, and worth engaging? — requires enough processing to distinguish a face from a background, a gaze direction from ambient movement, a co-wearer from a non-wearer. The sustained response is richer and slower, exactly as the frog's food-acquisition convexity detector is richer and slower than its predator-alarm dimming detector. A missed social connection is a lost opportunity. It is not death. The convexity detector's ε-threshold is higher — more specific, less trigger-happy — because its false positive cost (a socially awkward misfired introduction prompt) is larger than the dimming detector's false positive cost (a momentary privacy shield that wasn't needed).

**Col(F)₁ — Scene Registration (persistent; continuously maintained).** The scene model is not an urgency signal at all; it is the background process against which all urgency signals are computed. It runs continuously, at the lowest latency tier compatible with accurate SLAM registration, updating with every head movement and lighting change. Its conduction-velocity analogue is the unmyelinated sustained contrast fiber of the frog's retina — always active, always present, carrying the scene model that everything else is computed against.

**The 30:1 bandwidth allocation.** Approximately 30 waveguide optical channels are allocated to the sustained scene registration and social convexity operations (col(F)₁ + col(F)₂) for every one channel allocated to the transient motion alarm and privacy dimming operations (col(F)₃ + col(F)₄). The ecology of a socially mobile primate spending most of its time in sustained social-presence mode, with occasional needs for rapid privacy response, is encoded in this ratio — exactly as the ecology of the sit-and-wait ambush predator is encoded in the frog's 30:1 fiber ratio. The sustained operations receive the majority of the waveguide's information channel because the majority of a wearer's time is spent in sustained social engagement, not in emergency privacy responses.

The ε-threshold structure mirrors the conduction velocity hierarchy: the privacy shield fires to the smallest signal, the social convexity detector fires to the largest. The ecology of false positives and false negatives determines the calibration. The col(F)/ker(F) boundary is not a single threshold but a hierarchy of thresholds, each calibrated to the ecological cost structure of the operation it governs.
